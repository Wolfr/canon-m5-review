Canon EOS M5 review (kit)

Release date: november 2016 / Review date: August 2017 / Reviewed by: Johan Ronsse

The Canon M5 combines Canon's innovations from the past few years in a smaller package. This is a camera that you'll actually want to shoot with.

Review

Introduction/why did I buy this?

Recently I picked up a Canon M5 with an 18-150mm lens, with the following objectives:

  1. Own a portable camera that is better than my Sony RX100 III yet lighter than my Canon 80D. Basically something that I would be likelier to bring with me than a DSLR
  2. Own a 2nd camera for video work (so I can film for multiple angles simultaneously

Overall I am very happy with this purchase.

As a summary I would say that from a photography standpoint this is a pretty ultimate city trip camera, because of its flexibility and size; and because of the flexible range of the kit 18-150mm lens.

From a video standpoint it's strong for vlogging and travel videos because of its size and weight. It sits firmly in the mirrorless camera category where you have the advantage of lens flexibility, but the downside of a small sensor, which lowers the reliability in low light.

Canon's lens selection = meh?

Canon doesn't have nearly as much lenses as other manufacturers have for its M-series. This is something that's good to know, but also make sure to read about the adapter.

There's a 18-150mm, a 15-45mm, a 55-200m, an 11-22mm, a 22mm, and a 28mm. None of the lenses are particularly fast except maybe the 22mm which can open up to f/2.

For example, if you choose a Panasonic camera you can choose from the vast amount of micro 4/3 lenses out there. If you go for Fujifilm there's a vast amount lenses that can open up as wide as 1.4.

If your goal is to create fashion shots full of bokeh don't go for this camera, maybe go for Fujifilm, or even better - buy a full frame DSLR.

In-camera stabilization

The M5 features 5-axis image stabilisation when shooting movies, with increased image stabilization from the lens. In practice this means it's much easier to do stable handheld shots.

The secret control that wowed me

When digging through the options I found a way to choose focus on the touchscreen with your finger while still using the EVF to compose.

Basically when you enable this option and drag your finger over the screen you see a red dot in the EVF that shifts your focus point to where your finger is.

If you are a camera guy you know bothersome it is to do point selection with a directional pad. Now you just do it with your hand while composing the shot. Awesome.

The converter to use EF and EF-S lenses

There is a €100 converter that you can use to mount any EF or EF-S lens to an EF-M mount (EF-M is the mount type of the M series including the M5).

Attaching a heavy lens kind of defeats the purpose of having a lighter camera but if you already own Canon glass this definitely seems like a plus.

You can skip some body weight on shoots or use specialty lenses that you don't use much without having to buy them again in the EF-M format. For me it's useful to just have a 2nd camera that is different than the 80D but that I can use the same glass on.

If you own third party lenses like Sigma or Tamron lenses, I wouldn't count on the adapter to work as well as with Canon lenses. For example the adapter works with my Sigma 17-50mm lens but I think there's a software problem somewhere - its constantly focussing when it shouldn't, making so much noise to the point that I don't want to use this lens + body combination.

What about gimbals?

If you put the Canon EF-M 22m and a Rode Videomic micro on an M-5 your combined weight is very close to 800g. The recommended max. weight for a Zhiyun Crane-M is 850 g; so you're better to buy the regular Zhiyun Crane if you want to be able to use other heavier lenses as well.

I haven't tested any gimbals with the M5 yet but I am curious to try it in the near future.

Could it be used professionally?

With its price point the M5 falls right between enthusiast and professional.

In my opinion it could be used for professional work as a backup/2nd camera, especially for production work that just has to happen fast (e.g. social media posting).

Because of its size it's easy to carry everywhere - I can imagine this camera being a handy asset if your photo/video work involves a lot of travelling and you're capturing the travelling as you go along. Because of this it also seems like an excellent camera of choice for Youtubers/vloggers.

Why would you choose this over an 80D? Why over a compact camera?

I own a Canon 80D and it has the typical DSLR problems: too heavy, too conspicuous. You don't just throw it in your bag every day. You would only use it if you were shooting something specific i.e. for professional reasons. In the past I dragged my DSLR on travels but I can't imagine doing this anymore unless the purpose of the trip was specifically to make the best possible photos.

I also own a Sony RX100, which is great because I can literally put it in my back pocket and carry all the time. However, it's sometimes slow and basically you don't touch the settings because it's too much of a hassle.

The M5 sits in the middle, a bit more towards the DSLR spectrum maybe, where you have the flexibility of using different lenses, and just the right amount of top dials/controls to be able to do what you need.

A note on Canon's camera numbering

Canon's camera numbering is weird; in a Youtube video someone said that “what you have to know is that Canon's naming logic is that within a series, the lower the number, the better the camera” - but this is not true, since within the M series the 3 is a worse camera than the 5, the 6 is close to the 5 in terms of capabilities but has no EVF, and the 10 is something else altogether. Can anyone follow here?

Pros and cons

Pro

  • Right ergonomics; feels light yet sturdy
  • DLSR like quality in a small kit
  • Dual pixel AF is awesome
  • Drag focus on the touchscreen works great for focus pulling
  • Just the right amount of mode control
  • Option to use touch screen as a way to choose focus while still using the EVF
  • The 18-150mm is very flexible
  • Can use existing Canon lenses through adapter
  • Easy way to send photos to smartphone through WiFi or NFC
  • Touch screens helps to control the camera faster
  • Has a microphone input
  • Has a hot shoe mount for accessories such as a flash or an external flash trigger

Pro or con: it depends

  • Screen articulates to bottom of camera, making it better to do vlog-style footage, because you end up looking more natural looking down than up (most cameras have a screen that articulates up)
  • Screen articulates to bottom of camera, making it impossible to use it as a monitor when on a tripod
  • Small built-in flash - for me this is a con, it adds weight and bulk and I would probably almost never use it

Cons

  • Battery life
    • I had to order 2 extra batteries for my M5 (€100). After 1,5 hr of intense shooting the camera would already start to complain about low battery
    • Note that you can't use the Canon EOS M batteries, if you have a previous Canon that uses the LP E-12 batteries, you will have to get the new one (LP E-17). This is another €100 for 2.
  • Price
  • Struggles to get a good image in low light conditions
    • Don't throw away your crop or full-frame DSLR with low aperture lenses just yet.
    • To me there is no point in offering ISO12800/25600 if the image is unusable anyway
